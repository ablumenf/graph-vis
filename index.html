You can generate the following graphs:

  • $\ddot\smile$, the graph consisting of two copies of $K_4$, connected by one edge
  • $A_{100, 5, 100}$, where $A_{n,r,B}$ is defined to be $r$ copies of $K_n$ with $l_i \leq B$ edges connecting the $i$th copy of $K_n$ to the $(i+1)-$st copy of $K_n$ (since $l_i \leq B$, there is some randomness here)
  • $B$, an example network modeling an academic department. This has 1,130 vertices and 5,635 edges. The model is as follows: 10 administrative folks, 20 professors (each teaching a 50-student class), 100 grad students (20 are TAs), 1,000 undergrads. The administrative folks and professors form a $K_{30}$ subgraph. Each grad student knows 10 professors. Each undergrad knows his/her TA, and 20% of them know their professor. Each undergrad takes just one course (for simplicity). Under edge-repulsion linlog, the large obvious cluster that forms is everyone except the undergrads and the TAs.
  • $K_{250}$, the complete graph on $250$ vertices
  • Facebook graph from SNAP (which has $> 4,000$ nodes)
  • Undirected version of Twitter graph from SNAP (which has $> 80,000$ nodes)

Setting random means each graph model starts with randomly placed vertices. Setting biased means we feed FR with initial random placement, then feed the output into the input of node linlog, then feed that output into the input of edge linlog.

The Linlog energy models show clear clusters of vertices. One algorithm one could apply to actually assign cluster numbers to vertices is $k$-means. Note that both Linlog and $k$-means have the potential to suffer from poor local solutions. The paper also briefly mentions extensions of this algorithm to compute clustering assignments (which involve computing barycenters -- not too far off from the ideas of $k$-means).

The "naive" $O(n^2)$ algorithm is used. Barnes-Hut could be used to approximate solutions in $O(m + n\log{n})$ time. This would likely be necessary to look at any graphs significantly larger than the Facebook graph. In fact, even the Facebook graph is extremely slow to draw using the $O(n^2)$ algorithm given here. There are console log statements telling you which iteration you're on for this reason (100 iterations are used).

The edges, like in the paper, aren't drawn. The edges, other than with $\ddot\smile$, would likely clutter up the graphs and make them unreadable. The idea is to get a picture of the clusters, not to see every little detail of the graph. There's also the question of whether straight-line edges would be optimal, or some sort of arc, etc.

If you want to see graphs drawn with edges (for small enough graphs that you can type in the adjacency lists by hand), see here. Instructions on how to type in your graph are provided there.
